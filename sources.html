Section 06

Sources & Further Reading

Scholarly works, primary documents, and digital archives consulted in the preparation of this project. Citations in MLA format.

Category 01

Primary Sources

Legislative Document

Mississippi Legislature. "Laws of the State of Mississippi, Passed at a Called Session of the Mississippi Legislature, Held in Jackson, October, November and December, 1865." Jackson: J.J. Shannon & Co., State Printers, 1866. Reproduced by the Avalon Project, Yale Law School, avalon.law.yale.edu/19th_century/ms002.asp. Accessed 2024.

Legislative Document

South Carolina Legislature. "Acts of the General Assembly of the State of South Carolina, Passed at the Sessions of 1864–65." Columbia: Julian A. Selby, Printer to the State, 1866. Reproduced by the Freedmen and Southern Society Project, University of Maryland.

Government Report

Howard, Oliver Otis. "Report of the Commissioner of the Bureau of Refugees, Freedmen and Abandoned Lands." Washington D.C.: Government Printing Office, 1866. U.S. Congressional Serial Set, 39th Congress, 2nd Session, House Executive Documents.

Congressional Record

U.S. Congress. "Report of the Joint Committee on Reconstruction." Washington D.C.: Government Printing Office, 1866. 39th Congress, 1st Session. Includes testimony from freedpeople, Freedmen's Bureau agents, and Southern white witnesses regarding conditions in the former Confederate states.

Category 02

Scholarly Monographs

Book

Alexander, Michelle. The New Jim Crow: Mass Incarceration in the Age of Colorblindness. New York: The New Press, 2010. Print.

Foundational argument for understanding mass incarceration as a racially targeted system of social control that functions as a successor to Jim Crow. Chapter 1 provides essential historical context on the Reconstruction era, Black Codes, and convict leasing.

Book

Blackmon, Douglas A. Slavery by Another Name: The Re-Enslavement of Black Americans from the Civil War to World War II. New York: Doubleday, 2008. Print.

Pulitzer Prize-winning account of convict leasing and debt peonage. Provides extensive documentation of how vagrancy laws and labor statutes — direct descendants of the Black Codes — were used to re-enslave African Americans through the legal system. Essential reading.

Book

Du Bois, W.E.B. Black Reconstruction in America: An Essay Toward a History of the Part Which Black Folk Played in the Attempt to Reconstruct Democracy in America, 1860–1880. New York: Harcourt, Brace and Company, 1935. Print.

A landmark reexamination of Reconstruction from the perspective of African American agency and experience. Du Bois challenged the "Dunning School" interpretation that had portrayed Reconstruction as a failure caused by Black incompetence. Remains essential reading.

Book

Foner, Eric. Reconstruction: America's Unfinished Revolution, 1863–1877. New York: Harper & Row, 1988. Print.

The definitive modern history of Reconstruction. Foner's analysis of the Black Codes (chapters 5–6) is comprehensive and meticulously sourced. His framing of Reconstruction as "unfinished" provides the historical architecture for understanding why the Black Codes' legacy persists into the present day.

Book

Wilkerson, Isabel. Caste: The Origins of Our Discontents. New York: Random House, 2020. Print.

Situates American racial hierarchy within a global comparative framework of caste systems. Provides important context for understanding why racial control systems like the Black Codes are not aberrations but features of deeply embedded social hierarchies.

Book

Hine, Darlene Clark, William C. Hine, and Stanley Harrold. The African-American Odyssey. 6th ed. Upper Saddle River: Pearson, 2014. Print.

A comprehensive survey textbook of African American history that covers the Black Codes within the broader arc of American racial history. Useful for contextualizing specific provisions and their enforcement.

Category 03

Journal Articles & Academic Papers

Journal Article

Wilson, Theodore Brantner. "The Black Codes of the South." Journal of Southern History 37.2 (1971): 271–272. University of Alabama Press. Print.

A focused historical analysis of the legislative content and political intent of the Black Codes, with attention to variations between states and the response of Congress.

Academic Paper

Derenoncourt, Ellora, et al. "Wealth of Two Nations: The U.S. Racial Wealth Gap, 1860–2020." Quarterly Journal of Economics 139.2 (2024): 693–750. Oxford University Press. DOI: 10.1093/qje/qjad044.

Empirical economic study documenting the historical origins and evolution of the racial wealth gap, tracing its roots to the denial of asset accumulation opportunities to freed African Americans during and after Reconstruction.

Journal Article

Currie, David P. "The Reconstruction Congress." University of Chicago Law Review 75.1 (2008): 383–495. University of Chicago Law School. Print.

Legal-historical analysis of how Congress responded to the Black Codes through the Civil Rights Act of 1866 and the Reconstruction Amendments, with analysis of the constitutional frameworks used to challenge and dismantle the codes.

A Note on Sources

This project draws primarily on peer-reviewed historical scholarship and primary legislative documents. Where statistics are cited, sources are noted in the text. The interpretive framework — particularly the argument about historical continuity from Black Codes to mass incarceration — represents a scholarly consensus that has developed over several decades and is represented by the works cited above. This framework is not without debate in some particulars, but the broad argument for structural continuity is supported by substantial evidence across multiple disciplines.
