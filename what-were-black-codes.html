Section 01

What Were Black Codes?

Defining the laws that transformed emancipation into a conditional, circumscribed, and ultimately illusory freedom.

Definition

Laws Designed to Control

Black Codes were a collection of laws passed by Southern state legislatures in 1865 and 1866, in the immediate aftermath of the Civil War and the abolition of slavery. While these laws varied somewhat by state, they shared a common purpose: to restrict the civil rights, movement, labor, and economic autonomy of newly freed African Americans.

The term "Black Codes" itself reflects that these were laws written specifically — and often explicitly — to govern Black people and Black people alone. Many statutes contained direct racial classifications, applying only to "freedmen," "free Negroes," or "mulattoes." In states like South Carolina and Mississippi, entire legal chapters were dedicated to governing this population as a separate legal class.

Taken together, the Black Codes constituted a comprehensive legislative system that effectively negated the freedom that the 13th Amendment had legally granted. They controlled where Black people could live, what work they could perform, when they could travel, and what they could own — recreating, through the mechanism of law, a system of coerced labor and social subordination that closely mirrored slavery.

Though technically free, the former slaves found themselves enmeshed in a web of laws that returned them to a status barely distinguishable from their former bondage. The lash was replaced by the jail cell; the slave patrol by the local sheriff.
Douglas Blackmon, Slavery by Another Name, 2008
Historical Context

The Political Climate of 1865

Black Codes did not emerge in a vacuum. They were the product of a specific political moment — one defined by Confederate resistance, presidential leniency, and the urgent desire of white Southerners to restore antebellum social order.

April 1865

The Confederacy Falls

General Robert E. Lee surrenders at Appomattox Court House, effectively ending the Civil War. Approximately 4 million enslaved people become legally free. The Southern economy, dependent on enslaved labor, collapses.

May 1865

Presidential Reconstruction Begins

President Andrew Johnson announces lenient Reconstruction terms. Confederate states may rejoin the Union after ratifying the 13th Amendment and swearing loyalty — but face few other conditions. Former Confederate leaders quickly reclaim political power.

November 1865

Mississippi Leads the Way

Mississippi becomes the first state to enact a comprehensive Black Code, including sweeping vagrancy laws, mandatory labor contracts, and restrictions on property ownership. South Carolina follows within weeks.

December 1865

13th Amendment Ratified

The 13th Amendment is formally ratified, abolishing slavery throughout the United States. Yet within the same month, multiple Southern states have already begun passing laws designed to circumvent its implications.

1865–1866

Codes Spread Across the South

Alabama, Georgia, Louisiana, Florida, Texas, Virginia, and other former Confederate states pass their own versions of Black Codes. Congress grows alarmed at the reports coming from the South.

1866–1867

Congress Strikes Back

The Radical Republican-led Congress passes the Civil Rights Act of 1866 and the Reconstruction Acts of 1867, placing the South under military governance and formally nullifying the Black Codes. Enforcement, however, remains deeply uneven.

Mississippi and South Carolina enacted the most extensive and draconian codes. Mississippi's Black Code (November 1865) included mandatory labor contracts, apprenticeship provisions for Black children, sweeping vagrancy laws, and prohibitions on property rental outside of towns. South Carolina's code created separate employment categories — "servants" and "masters" — that replicated the terminology and structure of slavery.

Mississippi's Approach

Mississippi's Black Code was among the most comprehensive and severe. It included An Act to Confer Civil Rights on Freedmen — which sounded progressive but actually granted only the most minimal rights while imposing sweeping restrictions. It prohibited Black Mississippians from renting land in rural areas, required annual labor contracts, and criminalized unemployment as vagrancy.

South Carolina's Approach

South Carolina's code was notable for explicitly recreating the master-servant relationship. It forbade African Americans from working in any occupation other than farming or domestic service without paying an annual license fee that most could not afford. It also contained detailed regulations governing the behavior of "servants" toward their "masters."

Intent & Purpose

Why These Laws Were Passed

The Black Codes served several interconnected purposes for white Southern political and economic elites.

Purpose 01

Restore the Labor System

The plantation economy depended on a captive workforce. Without enslaved labor, planters needed a mechanism to compel African Americans to continue working the land at poverty-level wages. Vagrancy laws and mandatory contracts provided exactly that mechanism.

Purpose 02

Maintain Social Hierarchy

Southern white society was organized around a racial hierarchy with white supremacy at its center. Black Codes reinforced this hierarchy legally: restricting property ownership, prohibiting interracial marriage, banning jury service, and denying the right to vote.

Purpose 03

Prevent Political Power

Freed Black citizens represented a massive potential voting bloc. The codes worked to suppress this by denying suffrage, limiting assembly, restricting movement, and ensuring that Black Southerners remained economically dependent and politically powerless.

Purpose 04

Control Through Criminalization

By defining normal activities — walking without a job, renting rural land, assembling in groups — as crimes, Southern lawmakers created a permanent criminal underclass from the formerly enslaved population. Arrested individuals could then be "leased" to plantations as convict laborers.

Mississippi Black Code, 1865 — Preamble to the Vagrancy Act

"Be it enacted by the legislature of the State of Mississippi, That all freedmen, free negroes and mulattoes in this State, over the age of eighteen years, found on the second Monday in January, 1866, or thereafter, with no lawful employment or business, or found unlawfully assembling themselves together, either in the day or night time, and all white persons so assembling themselves with freedmen, free negroes or mulattoes... shall be deemed vagrants, and on conviction thereof shall be fined in a sum not exceeding, in the case of a freedman, free negro or mulatto, fifty dollars, and a white man two hundred dollars, and imprisoned..."

Source: Laws of the State of Mississippi, 1865. Reproduced in Yale Avalon Project.
